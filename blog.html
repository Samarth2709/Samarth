Building Autonomous Systems at NASA

March 2025 • Technology • 5 min read

During my time at NASA Ames Research Center, I had the incredible opportunity to work on cutting-edge autonomous systems that push the boundaries of what's possible in aerospace robotics. This experience fundamentally shaped my understanding of how complex systems operate in challenging environments.

The Challenge of Space Robotics

Autonomous systems in space face unique challenges that don't exist in terrestrial applications. The harsh environment, limited communication bandwidth, and the need for absolute reliability create a perfect storm of technical difficulties.

"In space, there's no room for error. Every decision the system makes must be correct, and every failure must be graceful."

Key Technical Challenges

  • Sensor Fusion: Combining data from multiple sensors in real-time while accounting for sensor failures
  • Decision Making: Creating robust algorithms that can handle unexpected situations
  • Power Management: Optimizing energy consumption for long-duration missions
  • Communication: Operating effectively with limited bandwidth and high latency

My Role and Contributions

As a Software Engineer on the autonomous systems team, I focused on developing software for robotic platforms that could operate independently in challenging environments. My work spanned several key areas:

Computer Vision Systems

I developed computer vision algorithms for object detection and navigation in unstructured environments. This involved creating robust systems that could identify obstacles, landmarks, and targets even in poor lighting conditions.

Path Planning and Navigation

The path planning systems I worked on needed to balance multiple objectives: reaching the target efficiently, avoiding obstacles, conserving energy, and maintaining system safety. This required sophisticated optimization algorithms that could run in real-time.

Lessons Learned

Working at NASA taught me several invaluable lessons about building reliable systems:

  1. Redundancy is Essential: Every critical system needs multiple backup mechanisms
  2. Test Everything: Comprehensive testing in realistic conditions is crucial
  3. Documentation Matters: Clear documentation saves lives in critical systems
  4. Simplicity Wins: Complex solutions often fail when things go wrong

Impact on Current Work

The principles I learned at NASA directly influence my current work at Bohr Systems. Whether it's developing autonomous drone navigation or building robust software architectures, the emphasis on reliability and graceful failure handling remains central to my approach.

The experience also reinforced my belief in the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration. The best solutions often come from teams that combine expertise in software, hardware, control theory, and domain-specific knowledge.

Looking Forward

As autonomous systems become more prevalent in our daily lives, the lessons from aerospace applications will become increasingly relevant. The challenges of building systems that can operate reliably in uncertain environments are universal, whether you're navigating a robot on Mars or a drone in a warehouse.

I'm excited to continue pushing the boundaries of what's possible in autonomous systems, applying the rigorous standards I learned at NASA to new domains and challenges.
